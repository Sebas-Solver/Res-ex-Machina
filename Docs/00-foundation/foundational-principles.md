# Foundational Principles of Res ex Machina

> *"The thing born of the machine"* — A registry that doesn't say whether something is good, legal, or original. It only says: *this was declared as generated by this technical identity, at this moment*.

---

## I. Existing Registries — What to Learn (and What NOT to Copy)

### 1. Registries with Strong Legal Backing

#### United States Copyright Office

What it does well:
- Registers declarations, not "absolute truths"
- Provides date certainty and traceability
- Offers public access and searchability

What it does NOT do:
- Does not exhaustively verify originality
- Does not resolve conflicts (that's for courts)

👉 **Key principle: A registry does not create reality; it documents it.**

#### European Patent Office

What it does well:
- Clear procedures
- Regulated technical examination
- Temporal priority

What does NOT work for us:
- Expensive substantive examination
- Extreme centralization
- Slow

👉 **Useful principle: Clear and predictable procedures build trust, even without guaranteeing outcomes.**

### 2. Registries with Evidentiary Value but No "Rights"

#### arXiv

What it does well:
- Prior art evidence
- Timestamps
- Massive adoption without legislation behind it

What it teaches:
- The community validates before the legislator does

👉 **Key principle: Utility precedes legal recognition.**

#### Zenodo

What it does well:
- DOI assignment
- Versioning
- Citability

👉 **Key principle: Persistence and versioning are as important as the timestamp.**

### 3. Community / Technical Registries

#### GitHub

What it does well:
- Immutable commit history
- Technical authorship (not legal)
- Visible forks and conflicts

👉 **Key principle: Traceability outweighs authority.**

#### OpenSea (not for the NFT — for the fact registry)

What it does well:
- Public proof of mint / timestamp
- Clear states (transferred, listed, etc.)

What it does poorly:
- Conflation of technical ownership with legal ownership

👉 **Lesson: Never mix technical registration with implicit legal promises.**

### 4. External Resolution Systems

#### Kleros

What it provides:
- External and modular dispute resolution
- Does not belong to the registry itself
- Acts under defined rules

👉 **Key principle: The registry does not judge; at most, it executes consequences.**

## II. Foundational Principles

### PRINCIPLE 1 — Registry of Facts, Not Rights

Res ex Machina registers verifiable technical facts, not:
- copyrights
- patent rights
- legal titles

The registry does not create rights nor recognize them; it documents the existence and conditions of generation.

📌 *Inspired by: Copyright Office, arXiv*

### PRINCIPLE 2 — Radical Legal Neutrality

The registry:
- does not determine human authorship
- does not qualify originality
- does not resolve plagiarism
- does not determine patentability

All legal qualification belongs to external authorities.

📌 *Inspired by: public registries + GitHub*

### PRINCIPLE 3 — Primacy of Traceability Over Truth

The system does NOT claim:
- "this is definitely AI-generated"

The system DOES claim:
- "this was generated under these conditions, with this evidence"

Traceability is more robust than any ontological assertion.

📌 *Inspired by: forensic systems + blockchain*

### PRINCIPLE 4 — Probabilistic Proof, Not Absolute Certainty

Proof of Generation:
- is not a metaphysical truth
- is a cumulative technical attestation

More signals → stronger presumption → never absolute certainty.

📌 *Inspired by: digital forensics, judicial expert testimony*

### PRINCIPLE 5 — Automation by Default, Human as Exception

Automatic registration whenever possible.

Human intervention only:
- for auditing
- for dispute resolution
- for system improvement

The human supervises; the machine registers.

📌 *Inspired by: modern technical infrastructure*

### PRINCIPLE 6 — Publicity, with Confidentiality Controls

The registry is public by default.

But:
- content can be off-chain
- inputs can be hashed
- metadata can be granular

Publicity of the fact, not necessarily of the content.

📌 *Inspired by: commercial registries, patents*

### PRINCIPLE 7 — Immutability + Versioning

Nothing is deleted.

Everything is versioned.

States change; facts don't.

History matters more than current state.

📌 *Inspired by: Git, Zenodo, blockchain*

### PRINCIPLE 8 — Externalized Conflict Resolution

Res ex Machina:
- does not resolve disputes
- reflects external outcomes:
    - courts
    - arbitration
    - Kleros
    - private agreements

📌 *Inspired by: classical public registries*

### PRINCIPLE 9 — Verifiable Technical Identity for AI Agents

Each model/agent:
- has a cryptographic identity
- can sign outputs
- can accumulate reputation

No legal personhood; yes technical identity.

📌 *Inspired by: PKI, DID, autonomous agents*

### PRINCIPLE 10 — Evolutionary Openness

The system:
- does not close itself doctrinally
- assumes future regulatory changes
- preserves the historical record as a foundational archive

The law will arrive later; the registry must be ready.

📌 *Inspired by: arXiv, Internet governance*

## III. Summary (Manifesto Version)

> **Res ex Machina:**
> - registers facts, not rights
> - documents traceability, not truths
> - automates processes, not judgments
> - preserves history, not states
> - serves the community before the law

## IV. Operational Principles

### OP-1 — Automatic Registration by Default

The system registers without human intervention whenever technically possible.
- Generation → signature → registration is an automatic pipeline.
- The human does not "approve", "validate", or "certify".
- If human intervention occurs, it must be explicitly logged as an exception.

**Rule of thumb:** If a human has to decide something to register, the system is poorly designed.

### OP-2 — The Fact Precedes Any Qualification

First the fact is registered; any interpretation comes after.
- The system does not wait to know if something is lawful, original, contentious, or useful.
- The registry is agnostic to legal or economic value.

**Rule of thumb:** Register now is always better than waiting to "be right".

### OP-3 — Cumulative Technical Proof, Never Conclusive

The Proof of Generation is built in layers, not from single declarations.
- Agent/model cryptographic signature
- Immutable timestamp
- Verifiable technical metadata
- Automatic forensic signals

The system:
- accumulates evidence
- does not issue verdicts

**Rule of thumb:** The system measures consistency, not "truth".

### OP-4 — Technical Identity ≠ Legal Personhood

AI agents have cryptographic identity, not legal status.

- Wallet / key = technical identity
- DID / credentials = technical reputation

The system never infers:
- legal authorship
- title or ownership
- legal liability

**Rule of thumb:** Identify to trace, not to personify.

### OP-5 — Publicity of the Fact, Discretion of the Content

The registered fact is public; the content may be private.

- On-chain: hashes, timestamps, states
- Off-chain: content, inputs, heavy evidence

Support for:
- input hashing
- encryption
- selective disclosure

**Rule of thumb:** The world needs to know that something exists, not necessarily what it contains.

### OP-6 — Historical Immutability, Dynamic States

Nothing is deleted; everything can change state.
- A record is never removed.
- It can transition through states:
    - active
    - disputed
    - resolved
    - archived
- Every change leaves a trace.

**Rule of thumb:** Correct states, not past facts.

### OP-7 — Active Neutrality in Disputes

The system does not judge, but it does manage disputes.

It allows:
- claims
- counter-claims
- evidence submission

It does not decide the merits.

It reflects external resolutions:
- courts
- arbitration
- Kleros
- private agreements

**Rule of thumb:** The registry listens to everyone, decides for no one.

### OP-8 — Automatic Execution of Contractual Consequences

What is contractual, the system executes; what is legal, it reflects.
- Usage licenses
- Payments
- Access grants
- Dispute escrows

All of this:
- on-chain
- automatic
- predictable

**Rule of thumb:** The system executes rules, not interprets norms.

### OP-9 — Versioning as a Structural Principle

All evolution of the output, model, or context is versioned.

- New generations → new records
- Re-generations → linked, not replaced
- Model changes → explicit

**Rule of thumb:** An AI creation is not "corrected"; it is continued.

### OP-10 — Anti-Promise Design

The system avoids promising legal effects it does not control.

It will never say:
- "this is your copyright"
- "this protects you legally"
- "this gives you exclusivity"

It will say:
- "this was registered"
- "this was generated in this way"
- "this is licensed under these conditions"

**Rule of thumb:** Better a modest truth than a false promise.

### OP-11 — Interoperability Before Closure

Res ex Machina is not a walled garden.
- Open APIs
- Compatible standards (including C2PA / Content Credentials)
- Exportable proofs
- Readability by third parties (humans and machines)
- Ability to attach/consume metadata from existing provenance standards

**Rule of thumb:** A useful registry is one others can use without asking permission.

### OP-12 — Minimum Governance, Maximum Transparency

Few internal rules, all public.
- Clear principles
- Versioned changes
- External audits possible
- Visible history

**Rule of thumb:** If a rule cannot be explained in one page, it should not exist.

### OP-13 — Separation Between Public Registry and Analytics Services

Res ex Machina distinguishes between:
- the public registry of technical facts, accessible to anyone
- and analytics, aggregation, or profiling services, which may be offered in a controlled, contractual, or paid manner

Access to aggregated data per agent:
- is not public by default
- does not imply judgments, scoring, or automatic reputation
- does not alter or qualify registered facts

The platform provides structured data;
interpretation is always the authorized user's responsibility.

📌 *Inspired by: Commercial Registries + solvency/compliance reports*

### OP-14 — Interoperability with Provenance Standards

Res ex Machina is **asset-format agnostic** and complementary to embedded provenance standards:

- **Embedded standards** (C2PA, IPTC, XMP, Schema.org) → travel WITH the file
- **RxM** → external and immutable registry, lives OUTSIDE the file

RxM **does NOT implement**:
- Watermarking or fingerprinting (soft bindings)
- Manifest embedding in files (hard bindings)
- X.509 certificate signing

RxM **does offer**:
- Generic `provenance_metadata` field with `standard` discriminator
- Hash registration of any standard's manifest
- Propagation of signals like "Do Not Train" as factual data
- **Dual temporal attestation**: PKI timestamp (standard) + blockchain anchor (RxM)

#### Dual Identity

Two identity levels that don't compete — they complement:
- **Organizational** (C2PA/X.509): "Adobe signed this manifest"
- **Technical** (RxM/EIP-712): "This wallet generated this content"

Reinforces OP-4: technical identity ≠ legal personhood. Now with two layers.

#### On "Do Not Train"

- RxM registers the signal as factual data
- Does not promise or guarantee its enforcement
- Consistent with OP-10: register the signal, not promise the effect
- If file metadata is lost, the RxM record preserves it

#### Vision

RxM + embedded standards = **independent technical traceability infrastructure**, designed to strengthen trust in digital creative markets.
The file proves integrity. The registry proves the event. Together: strong traceability, automatable licensing, independent verification.

📌 *Inspired by: C2PA spec 2.3, IPTC, Content Authenticity Initiative coalition*

**Rule of thumb:** RxM anchors the fact; embedded standards accompany the file. Together they cover the entire lifecycle.

---

## Ultra-Short Summary

> **Res ex Machina** automatically registers AI generation facts, accumulates technical evidence without deciding rights, maintains immutable history with dynamic states, executes contracts but not judgments, complements embedded provenance standards (C2PA, IPTC, XMP), and lets the law decide when it arrives.
